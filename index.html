II. Executive Summary

Bolivia regulates all games of chance, betting and lottery under Law No. 060 of 2010, enforced by the Autoridad de Fiscalización del Juego (AJ), a public authority under the Ministry of Economy and Finance.[1] The law creates a state‑controlled but concession‑based regime where only authorised operators may offer games, and all unauthorised gambling – including online – is expressly prohibited and subject to sanctions.[1][2]

The National Lottery of Charity and Health (Lotería Nacional de Beneficencia y Salubridad, LONABOL) operates under the Ministry of Health as the state lottery, organising national and regional draws that are supervised by the AJ.[3][4] Recent policy trends show both stricter enforcement – record fines and only one authorised casino – and a gradual opening to digital channels, with LONABOL launching its first official online ticket sales platform in 2026.[5][6]

III. Regulatory & Taxation Picture

Law No. 060 establishes the AJ as an autonomous regulatory body with nationwide jurisdiction to regulate, control and supervise lottery, games of chance, raffles and business promotions.[1][2][7] The AJ conducts inspections and audits, approves game modalities, supervises advertising and imposes fines, having levied record sanctions of over Bs 2.1 million on illegal operators in 2024 while overseeing just one licensed casino in Santa Cruz.[8]

Under secondary regulations and practice, lottery and other authorised games are typically subject to a national tax on Gross Gaming Revenue, with indicative reference rates in recent analyses showing around 15% GGR for lotteries, 30% GGR for casinos and 10% GGR for promotional games, in addition to general corporate income tax and municipal fees.[7] Online gambling remains largely unregulated under the current framework, though AJ has proposed amendments to formally include online verticals and an online control system (SICEL) to broaden the regulated offer.[4]

IV. Operating Environment & Retail Mechanics

Bolivia has a population of about 12.4 million, of which more than 70% live in urban areas, with the main cities providing the densest retail and payments infrastructure.[9][10][11] GDP reached roughly US$49.7 billion in 2024, and growth forecasts around 1.8–2.1% reflect a constrained but stable macro environment, where consumer spending is sensitive to price points and prize perception.[12][13]

Mobile penetration is high and supported by operators such as Tigo, whose Tigo Money product provides mobile wallets and a country‑wide agent network for bill payment and cash‑in/cash‑out services.[14][15][16] This infrastructure, together with QR‑based payments already adopted in LONABOL’s new digital ticket store, creates a workable backbone for hybrid offline‑online lottery distribution, while cash remains dominant at point of sale.[5][20]

V. Security, Compliance & Execution

AJ’s mandate and enforcement record make regulatory compliance and proper licensing central to any gaming initiative, with unlicensed lottery or games facing confiscation of equipment, fines and potential criminal implications under Law No. 060.[1][2][8] LONABOL’s long history as a social lottery under the Ministry of Health, and its draws being explicitly supervised by the AJ, underline the expectation that lottery instruments function as secure bearer documents with controlled printing, warehousing and draw procedures.[3][4][6]

Recent developments – including the launch of an ISO 27001‑aligned digital ticket sales platform and the proposed creation of an online control system (SICEL) for broader gambling – indicate an increasing focus on information security, traceability and real‑time reporting to the regulator.[4][5] Foreign investors partnering with LONABOL or other licensed entities will need to align logistics, customs management for printed tickets or terminals, and data hosting with AJ technical requirements, while leveraging local payment rails such as Tigo Money for settlement and prize payouts.
